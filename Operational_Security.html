1.2 Definition of Operational Security

By definition, operational security was derived from the military term procedural security, originated as a term that described strategies to prevent potential adversaries from discovering critical operations-related data. It is an analytical process that classifies information assets and determines the control required to provide these assets. Operational security encompasses both physical and digital aspects, as they are intertwined and cannot be separated.

What is Physical OPSEC?

Physical OPSEC, commonly referred to as analogue OPSEC, is about taking measures to protect yourself and your operations in the physical world. It involves practices such as securing your physical location, avoiding discussions about sensitive information, and minimizing the risk of being targeted or compromised.

People often tend to disregard physical OPSEC as less important, but make no mistake, it is as important as digital OPSEC. Just like in the digital world, I can only give you suggestions and make you think, as every situation and threat model is different.

Let's make an assumption that you're a dealer who prefers the old-fashioned way rather than using online markets. Here are some pieces of advice you might find useful:

  • Don't talk too much about your safe-house, the amount of product you have, or whether you're armed. Revealing such information can make you a target for abduction, torture, or even murder.
  • Don't "shit where you eat" â€“ avoid selling drugs in your own neighborhood. It's generally considered bad practice. Move your business to a different location.
  • Don't be friends with clients. You can't be friends with addicts. Addicts may cooperate with law enforcement if caught. Having an addict as a friend is a great way to ensure a long vacation at any correctional facility worldwide.
  • Know when to give up. Trust your gut. If something feels wrong, it probably is. Stepping back isn't always a bad thing.
  • Don't work with a "friend of a friend of a friend." They're probably undercover cops.
  • Always have an exit plan. Whether it's a forged passport and cash for a quick getaway or having enough funds to hire the best lawyer in the city, having a plan is essential.

These are just a few things to keep an eye out for. There is a wealth of more advice for various professions, but if I keep going, I won't be able to finish on time. Just remember that anything can be broken into, hacked, lock-picked, or exploited.

3.2 - Inevitable Fuck Up, Aftermath, and Clean-up

This is the last chapter of this post, discussing the inevitable fuck up and what to do afterward. We are all human, which means sooner or later, you will make a mistake. Here's a recurring example of a fuck up and how you may proceed afterward:

Controlled Delivery: If law enforcement seizes your order but allows the post to go ahead with delivering the parcel to catch you in the act, there are usually two outcomes. If you have several orders under your belt and it's taking suspiciously long for the parcel to be delivered, and it was stationed for days at the same place, you can either not know and sign for the package, getting busted within seconds, or you can deny receiving the package, in which case they have nothing.

If you suspect it's a controlled delivery, the best course of action is to remove any evidence of such activity from your devices and your home, as the address is likely compromised. Purging evidence involves using data shredders to securely erase data, but if there's critical information that must never fall into enemy hands, it's best to get rid of the SSD/HDD in question by shredding the data (recommended is at least 7 passes) and destroying the disk, such as by burning it. The point is, if something feels wrong, it probably is. Be vigilant, avoid ordering to your home address, and play the game without letting the game play you.
