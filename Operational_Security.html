1.2 - Definition of Operational Security

By definition, operational security was derived from the military term procedural security, originated as a term that described strategies to prevent potential adversaries from discovering critical operations-related data. It is an analytical process that classifies information assets and determines the control required to provide these assets. You might wonder why I choose to write about both physical and digital operational security? The simplest answer is they're intertwined and can't be separated in my opinion. If you have one but not the other, it's just like you don't have any.

2.2 - How are you being tracked

In today's digital age, we are constantly being tracked. From our online activities to our physical movements, various entities are collecting data about us. Here are some common ways you might be tracked:

  • Internet Browsing: Your internet service provider (ISP), websites you visit, and online advertisers can track your browsing history, search queries, and the content you interact with.
  • Social Media: Social media platforms collect vast amounts of personal data, including your likes, comments, shares, and connections. This information can be used to create detailed profiles about you.
  • Location Tracking: Mobile devices with GPS capabilities track your location data, which can be used to determine your movements and habits. Apps and services that request access to your location can also collect this information.
  • Cookies and Tracking Pixels: Websites often use cookies and tracking pixels to monitor your online behavior, track your visits, and serve targeted advertisements.
  • Online Purchases: When you make purchases online, your transaction history, payment details, and shipping address are collected and stored by e-commerce platforms.
  • Public Records and Data Brokers: Public records, such as property ownership and court records, are accessible to the public. Data brokers compile and sell personal information from various sources, including public records.

2.3 - How to use misinformation in your favor

Misinformation can be a powerful tool if used strategically. Here are a few ways to utilize misinformation to your advantage:

  • Disinformation Campaigns: Spread false information to confuse or deceive your adversaries. This can be done through social media, fake news websites, or anonymous sources.
  • Misdirection: Create a diversion by deliberately leaking false information or leading others to focus on a decoy while you carry out your actual plans.
  • Manipulating Public Opinion: Influence public perception by disseminating misinformation that supports your agenda or undermines your opponents. This can be achieved through targeted messaging and social media manipulation.
  • Psychological Operations: Use psychological techniques to exploit cognitive biases and manipulate individuals' beliefs and behaviors. Misinformation can be tailored to appeal to specific emotions or exploit existing divisions in society.

However, it's important to note that using misinformation ethically and responsibly is crucial. Unchecked spread of false information can have severe consequences and harm innocent individuals.

2.4 - Compartmentalization

Compartmentalization is a key principle in operational security. It involves dividing information, tasks, and responsibilities into distinct compartments to minimize the risk of unauthorized access or compromise. By compartmentalizing information, even if one compartment is breached, the damage can be contained, and the overall security can be maintained.

Compartmentalization can be applied in both physical and digital contexts. In physical security, it may involve restricted access areas, secure storage of sensitive documents, and a need-to-know basis for classified information. In digital security, it may include access controls, encrypted communication channels, and separating systems or networks based on their levels of sensitivity.

2.5 - Security is not convenient

Maintaining operational security requires effort and sacrifices. It often involves implementing security measures that may inconvenience individuals. This could include using strong and unique passwords, regularly updating software and firmware, enabling multi-factor authentication, and being cautious about sharing personal information online.

While these measures may seem burdensome, they are necessary to protect against threats and maintain the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of sensitive information. It's important to prioritize security over convenience to safeguard both your personal and organizational interests.

Section 3 - Physical Operational Security (D.U.M.B.)

Physical operational security, commonly referred to as "analogue" OPSEC, is an essential aspect of overall security. It encompasses measures taken to protect physical assets, information, and operations from unauthorized access, theft, or compromise. In the context of this section, the acronym D.U.M.B. stands for Deep Underground Military Bases, symbolizing the concept of impenetrable security.

It's important to emphasize that both digital and physical security are equally significant. Neglecting one aspect can undermine the effectiveness of the other. To gain a comprehensive understanding of the importance of physical security, I recommend exploring the works of Jayson Street, such as "Dissecting the Hack: F0rb1dd3n Network" and "Dissecting the Hack: STARS (Security Threats Are Real)." These books provide valuable insights into the significance of both digital and physical security and the consequences of disregarding either.

Additionally, "The Complete Book of Locks and Locksmithing, Seventh Edition" and "Master Locksmithing: An Expert's Guide" are informative resources that delve into the world of locks, offering a trove of knowledge for enthusiasts.

Now, let's explore what physical OPSEC entails and why it holds immense importance. Analogue OPSEC refers to practices that involve protecting physical aspects of security, much like ensuring your digital presence is secure. It encompasses actions such as not leaving your logged-in device unattended while using markets to order drugs or ensuring your house doors are locked when you're away. These seemingly basic measures are crucial for maintaining security.

Unfortunately, physical OPSEC is often underestimated compared to its digital counterpart. However, it is equally vital in safeguarding sensitive information and operations. While I can provide suggestions and food for thought, it's important to note that every situation and threat model is unique. With that in mind, let's assume you are a dealer who prefers the traditional method rather than utilizing markets. Here are some useful pieces of advice:

  • Keep your safe-house location and details to yourself: Avoid sharing information about the whereabouts of your safe-house, the quantity of drugs you possess, whether you're armed, and similar details. Revealing such information can make you a target for abduction, torture, or even murder.
  • Don't conduct business in your own neighborhood: It's generally ill-advised to engage in drug sales in your immediate community. This practice, commonly referred to as "shitting where you eat," can have detrimental consequences. Instead, consider moving your operations to a different part of town to minimize risks.
  • Avoid befriending clients: It's important to draw a clear line between being a dealer and forming friendships with your clients. Addicts, in particular, can pose a significant risk, as they may cooperate with authorities if caught. Remember that addicts cannot be both friends and reliable business partners. Associating with addicts increases the likelihood of ending up in a correctional facility.
  • Trust your instincts and know when to step back: Trusting your intuition is crucial. If something feels wrong, it probably is. Don't hesitate to step back from a situation that raises suspicions. As Frank Lucas was advised by his supplier, "Giving up and giving up while you're ahead is not the same, Frank."
  • Avoid working with friends of friends: Working with individuals connected through multiple degrees of separation can be risky, as they may be undercover law enforcement officers. Exercise caution and conduct thorough background checks before engaging in business relationships.
  • Plan ahead and have an exit strategy: Always be prepared by having an exit plan in place. This could involve having a forged passport, a substantial amount of cash, and a ticket to a remote South African island without extradition treaties. Alternatively, it could be securing the services of an expensive lawyer with a down-payment of a few hundred thousand dollars. The key is to have a plan ready for contingencies.

These are just a few considerations to keep in mind, and there are numerous other pieces of advice applicable to various professions. However, sharing all of them would require more time. Remember that anything can be breached, hacked, lock-picked, or exploited. Exercise caution, think ahead, and implement robust security measures.

3.2 - Inevitable Fuck Up, Aftermath, and Clean-Up

The final chapter of this post addresses the inevitable scenario of making a mistake and provides guidance on how to proceed afterward. As humans, we are prone to errors, and it's crucial to know how to clean up the mess we create. Here's a recurring example of a fuck-up and the possible steps to take afterward. Please note that this is a speculative situation, and I cannot predict every possible outcome.

Controlled Delivery: A controlled delivery occurs when law enforcement seizes your illicit order but allows the postal service to proceed with delivering the package to catch you in the act. In such a situation, there are usually two outcomes. First, if you have previous orders and notice that a package is taking suspiciously long to arrive, staying stationary at a particular location for days, it might indicate a controlled delivery. In this case, you have two choices: either unknowingly accept the package and get caught within seconds, or deny receiving the package, leaving law enforcement with no evidence.

If you suspect it's a controlled delivery, the best course of action is to remove any evidence of your illicit activities from your devices and your home. Assume that your address is compromised and act accordingly. To purge evidence, use data shredders to securely erase files. For critical information that must never fall into enemy hands, consider getting rid of the relevant SSDs/HDDs. First, securely erase the data (recommended with at least seven passes), and then physically destroy the disk. Burning the disk to a crisp is usually effective. Always prioritize your freedom over any material possessions, including shiny new devices like laptops, computers, HDDs, SSDs, etc. Because no new shiny device (laptop, computer, HDD, SSD, etc.) is worth more than your freedom. Remember, the ultimate goal is to protect yourself and avoid compromising your security. If something feels wrong, trust your instincts, as it is likely because something is indeed wrong. Stay vigilant and adopt a cautious approach.

When engaging in illicit activities, it's crucial not to order items to your home address. Instead, adopt strategies such as using anonymous drop points or utilizing different addresses for each transaction. By doing so, you can minimize the risk of law enforcement tracing activities back to your physical location. In this game, it's essential to play smart and not let the game play you.

Maintain a proactive and adaptive mindset, continually assessing your security measures and adapting them as necessary. Stay informed about emerging threats and security practices to stay one step ahead. Remember, your safety and freedom are paramount. By prioritizing physical and digital security, and by employing sound operational security practices, you can enhance your chances of maintaining anonymity and avoiding detection.
